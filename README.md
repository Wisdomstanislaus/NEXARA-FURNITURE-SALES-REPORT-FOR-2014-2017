NEXARA Furniture Sales Performance Report (2014–2017)
Project Overview
This report presents a structured pre-analysis of Nexara Furniture’s sales performance from 2014 to 2017. The dashboard consolidates revenue, profitability, quantity sold, shipping patterns, geographic performance, product category contributions, customer segmentation, and order behavior to provide a comprehensive business performance overview.
Aim
To evaluate overall sales performance, profitability drivers, operational patterns, and geographic trends in order to identify growth opportunities, operational risks, and strategic priorities.
Objectives
Assess total revenue and profit performance.
Identify peak-performing months and years.
Evaluate product sub-category contribution to revenue.
Examine regional and city-level sales distribution.
Analyze shipping mode utilization and order duration patterns.
Assess profitability across customer segments.
Identify operational inefficiencies and revenue concentration risks.
Hypothesis
Sales performance improved over time, peaking in 2017.
Certain product sub-categories disproportionately drive revenue.
December contributes significantly to annual sales.
Profit margins may be compressed relative to total sales.
Corporate and Consumer segments contribute most to profit.
Shipping and delivery duration patterns may impact operational efficiency.

Key Insights
1. Profitability is Critically Low
With $741,722 in sales but only $18,463.33 in profit, the profit margin is approximately 2.49%. This indicates margin compression, heavy discounting, high operational costs, or inefficient pricing strategy.
2. Strong Q4 Seasonality
Sales surge significantly in September ($106,383), November ($121,285), and December ($121,820). Q4 drives a disproportionate share of revenue, indicating heavy seasonal dependence.
3. Chairs Dominate Revenue Mix
Chairs contribute $328,172 (largest share), accounting for roughly 44% of total revenue. Revenue concentration risk exists if demand declines in this category.
4. Geographic Revenue Concentration
New York City alone generates $75,698, significantly ahead of other cities. The lowest cities generate under $10, indicating extreme geographic imbalance.
5. Standard Class Dominates Shipping
1,247 orders use Standard Class, representing the majority of shipments. Operational efficiency heavily depends on this shipping method.
6. 4-Day Orders Are Most Frequent
600 orders take 4 days, the highest duration count. This suggests 4-day delivery is the operational norm.
7. Corporate Segment Most Profitable
Corporate ($7,584.8) slightly outperforms Consumer ($6,991.1). This suggests B2B relationships may be more margin-stable.
8. February Underperformance
February records only $15,770, the lowest monthly sales, indicating demand weakness post-holiday season.
9. Revenue Concentration Risk
Top cities generate tens of thousands, while bottom cities generate single-digit revenue — indicating inefficient market penetration or inactive markets.
10. High Volume, Low Return
8,026 units sold with minimal profit indicates potential pricing inefficiencies or high cost of goods sold.
Operational / Business Implications
Margin sustainability is at risk with sub-3% profitability.
Q4 dependency increases vulnerability to seasonal disruptions.
Overreliance on Chairs exposes product concentration risk.
Market expansion opportunities exist in underperforming cities.
Shipping optimization could significantly impact cost structure.
Corporate segment may offer scalable B2B growth opportunities.
Challenges & Risks Identified
Low profit margin (2.49%).
Revenue concentration in limited cities.
Over-dependence on Q4 performance.
High reliance on one sub-category.
Potential shipping inefficiencies.
Weak early-year sales (Q1 softness).
Strategic Recommendations
Conduct margin analysis per sub-category, especially Chairs and Tables.
Introduce targeted Q1 promotions to reduce February slump.
Expand corporate contracts and bulk pricing strategies.
Reassess pricing strategy to improve margin percentage.

Launch geo-targeted campaigns in underperforming cities.
Diversify product portfolio to reduce revenue concentration risk.
Investigate cost drivers impacting profitability.
Implement profitability tracking per city.
Strengthen Q4 inventory planning to maximize peak season returns.

Conclusion
Nexara Furniture demonstrates strong revenue generation capability ($741,722) and healthy sales volume (8,026 units), but profitability remains critically low ($18,463.33). The business is heavily reliant on Q4 performance, Chairs as a dominant revenue driver, and key metropolitan markets.
To ensure sustainable growth, the company must prioritize margin optimization, geographic expansion, operational efficiency, and revenue diversification. Without strategic intervention, revenue growth alone may not translate into meaningful profitability improvement.
